First Contact

First contact with the policyholder is one of the most important phases of the claims process.  Letting the policyholder know you are their adjuster and that you are there to help them is critical.  We expect you to contact the policyholder as soon as possible after receiving the assignment (no later than 48 hours).  Most if not all policyholders want to be seen immediately.  Unfortunately, someone will be first and someone will be last, and it is your job to make every policyholder feel important.

Please do not share too much information with the policyholder concerning your claims handling process.  Telling the policyholder how you make your decision who to see first and who to see in a few days sometimes makes the policyholder feel less important and generates complaint calls to the carrier.

The policyholder has no way of knowing if they are your first appointment or your tenth.  Although in a very large event, we sometimes need to schedule appointments out for weeks in advance, you need to listen to the policyholder and sometimes move the “screamers” up on the list.  Telling an already upset policyholder it will be three weeks before you can see them will only generate a phone call to the carrier and/or FEMA, the DOI and their congress person.  Evaluating the policyholder is part of being a good adjuster.  When the policyholder calls the carrier, and the carrier calls our office, we will either have you reschedule, or we will be forced to reassign the claim.  In extreme cases, the claims have been pulled from Fountain Group.  Please pay close attention to how the policyholder responds to your call and make the right decision on setting appointments.

Please never share that you are not already in the area.  The policyholder does not know where you are and really does not care.  Also, if you leave the area, do not share that information either.  (We will discuss later our thoughts on when it’s appropriate to leave a storm sight.)

Preliminary Report

All Prelims are due within 24 hours of inspection.  We would prefer that they be submitted the same day as the inspection, when possible. You should be using the Venue App for all Prelims.

Make sure to include a current copy of your FCN card.  Mentees should also include a copy of their mentor’s card.  This is automatic in the Venue App.

Nearest body of water- should be the actual nearest body of water like a creek or canal…not the river 2 or 3 miles away.  NFIP GAs actually google map them to confirm our information. In some cases, it will not be the source of the actual flooding.

Include the policyholder's phone number(s) and email address.

Make sure the depth on prelim matches the photos.

Water depth photos must have a visible water line, flood debris, or something confirming how high the water actually was.

Also remember on photo#1…We want the FIRM (pre or post), Elevated, Non-elevated, or basement, and the water Depths (interior & Exterior), as well as the duration of the flood and the flood Zone.

Cat Code (EDN) needs to be on prelim if applicable.

We must include tape measurement photos of both the exterior and interior water lines. If you can’t find a water line, still take a photo of the tape measure and put a pen or clothes pin at the estimated water depth.   Your photos comments should discuss how you made this estimation.

Private Flood/Excess Policy Claims

Please do not put the Cat Number (EDN) or your FCN number on your Private Flood reports.  Your State License Number should be used on all the forms.  Make sure you are using the non-NFIP forms for your reports. NFIP/FEMA should never be mentioned on Private Flood Claims.

Advances

Advances and Prelims should be uploaded separately. The advance payment form can be included with the Prelim report package (stack), but should also be uploaded separately in Venue as well.  When using the Venue app, the advance is automatically uploaded separately.

The Advance form package should include your Preliminary Report, FCN Card and Prelim Photos. (Prelim package (stack))

Advance Photographs: Your photos must support the amount of your advance request.  If you are requesting a contents advance, your photos need to show an adequate amount of damaged contents to support the advance amount taking into consideration depreciation and the deductible.

No Advance: If you do not request an advance, you must explain why in your prelim report.  (FCN Card page or Photo 1 comments) “No advance requested” is not a valid reason. You need to explain why you are not requesting an advance.  Examples: Prior loss, small loss with large deductible, PFE Limitations, or that the policyholder was not present or declined.

Progressive

Progressive would like to keep the advances to $10,000.00 - $15,000.00 range (no more than 20% of the reserves)

If the building is a total loss, we can consider a higher advance.  (case by case)

*** Also, please be aware that Progressive has published their expectations on their claims, and we have included a copy in Venue Instructions.

Wright/National General/Liberty Mutual/Selective/NFIP Direct/Bankers/Tower Hill (Basically, all other clients)

Advance up to 25% of the reserve amounts for any size loss.  This should be the maximum requested, please check your math.

Bankers

Requires that we use their advance form. (Included in Venue Instructions) However, Bankers has agreed that the advance form submitted with the Venue app is acceptable.

All Companies (please consider before you request an advance)

Prior loss/coverage issues; reduce the advance amount accordingly, or do not offer one at all.

Be careful advancing on contents.  Please ensure that the contents loss will exceed the amount of the advance keeping in mind depreciation and deductible.  If you request a contents advance, make sure to include photos showing damaged contents.

*If there was a prior loss and you are requesting an advance, you must include a short, captioned report (Status Style) advising the carrier that you believe based on your inspection or documents submitted by the policyholder, that all or most prior loss repairs were made.  If the prior loss repairs were not made, we should not be requesting an advance.  Please use good judgment.

Status Reports

Status reports are REQUIRED every 30 days for all open claims including any open supplemental (RAP) claims.  The 30 days start the day you submit the Preliminary Report or the day you open the RAP.

We also need to start updating the reserves on every Status Report.  Especially, if you have written the estimate and are waiting on the policyholder, you should update the reserves.  If you have not written the estimate and feel the original reserves are still good, just mention that as well.  The reserve amount should be the net amount you expect to recommend (Payment amounts).  On RAP claims, it would only be the net supplemental amount.  (Payment amount)

We cannot express how important this is. We will be monitoring status reports just like prelims and finals because our clients monitor them all the time as well.

When submitting Wright status reports, make sure to use the correct format.

Mortgage Company

It’s our job, as the adjuster, to verify the mortgage company listed on the loss notice is correct.  If during your initial conversation with the policyholder you discover the information is incorrect, you need to obtain the correct information and advise the company as soon as possible.  Do not wait until you are closing the claim to submit this information.  Do it as soon as possible.

If the lien has been paid off, then documentation to support the payoff will be required to remove the lien holder from the check.  If the lien has been sold or transferred, you need to document the change.  You need to document how the loan moved from one lienholder to the next.  If you only provide the new lienholder information, the company may/will think the new lienholder is a 2nd lien and put both lienholders on the check.  You need to document the change/transfer, just having the new name will not suffice.

Please make sure to review the Flood Survey as many times the policyholder has put down this information while completing the form.  If the Flood Survey indicates a different lien holder, the claim payment could/will be held up.

Also, if you discover there is a second or third lien against the property, we must get that information as well and notify the company.  The NFIP is obligated to protect the interests of these lien holders.

Prior Loss Requests

If the loss notice indicates there was a prior loss, you should immediately request the prior.  If during your initial phone call or inspection you discover there was a prior loss that was not indicated on the loss notice, you need to request the prior.  Make sure to review the survey as the policyholder may indicate the prior loss on the survey.

Please check Venue Docs for the prior before making your request.  In some cases, we handled the prior and are able to provide the information.

Upload the request for the prior in a status report format including the date of the prior loss.  Just requesting a prior without providing the prior DOL generally will not work.  The more information you can provide, the easier and quicker it will be to get the prior loss.   Sometimes we will not know the date and sometimes the prior was in another name (property sold after prior flood).  Again, the more information you can provide the better.

GETTING STARTED ON FINALS!

Let’s discuss moving forward from the prelim phase on to the finals phase. We will be watching your closing production just as much as we have the prelim production. We will do everything we can to help you close your claims as quickly as possible without any issues, but ultimately the rate of production lies on your shoulders which in turn can either help or hurt your position with FG.

We have a narrative report template. Keep in mind this is just a template with suggestions on what should be included. Every claim is different and the information in the narrative should be adjusted to fit the details of each claim. PROOF READ the narrative every time! We see spelling and grammar mistakes in almost every report. If an examiner has trouble reading your narrative report, they are likely to spend additional time on their review of your entire claim file including the estimate. Pay attention to the details!  (Consider using AI to improve your reports)  The truth of the matter is that many examiners will look at a detailed report and only skim over your estimate.  However, if the narrative does not make sense, they will scrutinize your estimate.

There is a hierarchy of claims you should follow in order to have the fastest production (Closure) rate. This is IMPORTANT!   Don’t wait until you are fully inspected to start closing claims. If you have claims you can close, close them immediately.  You should complete the smallest and least complicated claims first. These include erroneous claims, CWOP’s (closed without payment), estimates below deductible, low limits claims, and claims well over the policy limits.  Also, claims with no content’s coverage should be closed quickly. If you have a handful of these claims and submit them right away, not only are you helping your numbers look good, but you are also helping FG's overall numbers look good. The end goal is to provide a quality product for our clients as quickly as reasonably possible. This in turn will be very profitable for all involved!  This affects your future and FG’s future.  If we start closing claims quickly, the companies are more likely to assign FG more claims.  And, FG assigning you more claims.

As you know, claims practices sometimes change.  So, we suggest that if you haven't worked flood claims for FG in over a year, that you submit a few (2 to 3) final reports first for us to review and critique. This way you will only have to correct any issues on a few claims.  Do not wait and submit 10 or 12 claims at one time.  You should be submitting claims as you complete the paperwork.

Engineer Request

Engineers need to be requested as soon as possible.  Requests made 30 or 60 days after the inspection are a concern to both FG and Carrier Management.  If the policyholder is claiming damage to the foundation (or any structural component) of the risk, during your initial inspection, we need to consider making the request immediately.

If you are not sure whether to make the request or not, please contact FG management as soon as possible.   There are times when the damage is not noted during the inspection or is hidden and only after items are torn out is it found.  In these cases, we need to explain in our request why the request was delayed and make the request as soon as possible after receiving this new information.

As the adjuster, it is your decision to request the engineer, not the policyholder’s choice.  There must be some type of physical evidence to support your request, not just the policyholder requesting an engineer because they want to make sure there is no structural damage.

Your request should include what the policyholder is claiming, what evidence of loss you noted during your inspection (or lack of damage) and include photos to support these assertions.

If the policyholder is claiming foundation movement/cracks and there is evidence of these cracks and/or movement, you must take photos of the perimeter of the risk (all sides) and include them with your request.

As you know, earth movement is excluded by the policy and only under specific conditions (Definition #2 of Flood) is coverage afforded.  Even if the earth movement is caused by flood, the damage may not be covered by the policy.

We do not have the authority to hire an engineer.  We can only request that the WYO/DSA make that request.  Please do not tell a policyholder that an engineer is going to be hired, only say that you will make the request.

Do not tell the policyholder to hire their own engineer.  In most cases, if the policyholder hires an engineer and the report indicates damage caused by flood, the WYO/DSA will hire their own engineer.  The policyholder “may” hire their own engineer if they choose, but we should not suggest that they do so.  Most reports obtained by the policyholder hired engineers fails to provide enough information to determine if the damage is caused by a covered loss or not.  Also, generally the policyholder would not be reimbursed for their costs hiring an engineer.

If there is possible flood damage to the foundation or possible structural damage to the risk, we can make the request. (and should make the request)

Ceramic Tile: All carriers are going to want an engineer or Tile Expert (Wright Flood) to inspect to determine if flood has caused damaged to ceramic tile floors installed directly on a slab.   Ceramic tile over subfloors should not require an engineer if damage is evident.

Estimating Issues

Debris removal

If the policyholder placed their damaged building and contents items at the curb and the city/county are picking up that debris, we do not need to include dumpsters for that debris.  However, we do need to allow dumpster(s) for the reconstruction debris.  The size of the job will determine how many will be needed.  The adjuster must add a note under the line item: “Reconstruction Debris”.

If the city/county is not picking up the debris, or if there is a dumpster on site being used or the policyholder provides actual invoices for the dumpster, then we can include dumpsters for demo debris.  Please make sure you discuss in your narrative report.

Wright Flood: In the narrative report include a similar comment discussing what you are allowing the dumpster for.

Torrent Companies (Excluding Bankers, First Community and Selective):  We need to provide a receipt for the dumpster to include.

Sales Tax

Building Items

Sales tax should be added to Material and Equipment.  Not to Labor, overhead and Profit. (unless we are in a State that requires tax on Labor, this should be documented)

Contents Items

Our estimate should reflect the addition of sales tax to the totals.

Adjuster Invoice

Sales tax should be added to our adjuster fee for all claims in Texas.  Should be a separate line item: Texas Tax 8.25% is the rate we should use.  Make sure you are using the current fee schedule.

BACKER BOARD (Sheathing)

This is an issue on many events.  When homes are severely flooded and the exterior sheathing/backer board was submerged for hours and sometimes days, replacement may be required.

If the brick veneer is structurally sound, we can pay to remove and replace the sheathing/backer board from the interior without requesting an engineer.

We need to see (identify) visible flood damage to the sheathing.  Our photos must show this damage.  If we cannot show damage, we cannot include replacement of the sheathing.   Our report does not need to address the class of sheathing affected.  However, if damaged and included in our estimate we should address the appropriate materials and repair process in our estimate.

Sheathing replacement (Brick Veneer)

We should consider replacing the damaged sheathing from the interior versus the exterior if we are dealing with a brick veneer building.  (please get with management for pricing)

If the policyholder’s contractor advises the brick veneer must be removed to facilitate repairs to the backer board, you need to discuss with management.  We may need to request an engineer to inspect and make that determination.

If you are replacing the backer board, the line item in your estimate should specify what type of material we are replacing; plywood sheathing, OSB sheathing, or gypsum backer board.  You should include two separate line items, one to remove and one to replace.  This should be in the exterior portion of your estimate, not room by room.

IE:

Remove Exterior Plywood Sheathing

Replace Exterior Plywood Sheathing OR

Remove Gypsum Backer Board

Replace Gypsum Backer Board

Sheathing replacement (wood, aluminum, and vinyl siding)

If we determine the sheathing is damaged and we are dealing with a risk with some type of siding, we need to consider removing and resetting the siding rather than replacement from the interior of the risk. See Wood Siding below.

Wood Siding

Depending on the type of siding, its age, its condition and the length of time the risk sat in flood waters, it may be necessary to replace the wood siding on the exterior (to just above the water line in some cases).  Please use good judgment and explain why you are recommending what you are recommending.   Also, include photographs to support your conclusion/estimate.

Our estimate should reflect the most cost effective (but proper) repair costs.

Electrical wiring

If the receptacles and/or switches were submerged in flood waters, we can include the replacement of the wire run back to its last connection (Junction box) as well as the outlet/switch.

If the breaker box was submerged, a complete re-wire of the risk can be considered.  If a total rewire is required, please us the correct pricing for replacement taking into consideration what is included in the operation.  IE: Don’t include items like fixtures that are not damaged by flood.

Please remember the flood policy does not cover any required code upgrades.

Granite and solid surface countertops

If the flood water was deep enough to reach the countertops and if at the time of your inspection the countertops had been removed and discarded, we should be able to consider they were damaged by flood waters.  Also, if the policyholder informs us they broke during the attempt to removal them, we may also be able to consider them flood damaged as we can argue the countertops have direct physical loss by or from flood.   (Again, if touched by flood waters)

If the flood waters did not reach (no direct physical damage) the countertops, we should always default to paying for removal and resetting (This applies to all types of countertops).  If the countertops broke during the removal process, then we need to document what efforts were made to remove and reset the countertop before considering replacement.  In the case of granite or other solid surface, we need to get a written statement from the contractor that explains why they broke and we need photos of the broken countertops to support the claim.

If the counter tops are no longer available for inspection, the policyholder would still need to prove they had the type (material) of countertop being claimed.  If they broke after you inspected, they must provide photos to prove their claim.

Please use good judgment and the facts about the claim. If the policyholder’s contractor broke the tubs, commode and all counter tops during removal, caution may not have been exercised during the countertop removal which may be the cause of the damage.  We do not owe for avoidable damage caused by the contractor.

Granite countertops that have been completely submerged in flood waters are to be considered damaged and we can/should include replacement.  However, if the policyholder has saved the counter tops and are in fact going to reuse them, then we would pay for cleaning, removal and resetting only.

If the countertop is damaged during the removal process (see below), we may be able to recommend the replacement of the countertops.  Use good judgment, if the contractor used a sledgehammer to remove the cabinets, we may not owe for the counter tops.

Our allowance for removing and resetting the countertop is an allowance for a professional to do the work.  If the contractor uses their demo crew and they are not qualified to remove the countertops, then the damage may have been avoidable and therefore not covered by the program.

Please take good photos of the countertops, including shots from inside the cabinets if possible.  We will need the thickness documented as well.

Please refer to the claim’s manual for specific instructions for countertops.

Policyholder Documentation

The policyholder can best support a request for payment when unavoidable damage occurs during the removal and replacement process with:

• Clear photographs of the damage at the time of occurrence; and

• A signed detailed statement from the removal or repair contractor. The signed detailed statement must:

– Explain the action taken to remove the item; – State how the damage occurred; and

– Address salvage.

A/C Units (outside condensing units/heat pumps, Package units, etc.)

If the A/C unit is completely submerged, we can consider replacement without the need for an HVAC inspection.  This would not apply if the unit is running at the time of your inspection.  If the flood waters only partially submerged the unit, then a technician may still be required.   We would recommend that you follow the following rule of thumb.  If the water is over the fan blades, recommend replacement, if it is below the fan blades, have a technician inspect the unit.  (On salt water claims, we should consider replacement if the unit has more than 12” of water and/or it reaches the electrical components.)

Make sure you are allowing for the correct size unit.  Generally, the size can be determined by the model number.  Most manufacturers embed the size of the unit in their model number.  IE: WA03234847G would be a 4 ton condenser. The embedded number will be devisable by 12.   48/12 = 4 ton, 42/12 = 3.5 ton, 30 = 2.5 ton.    The size of the house can also help confirm your estimation.   You need approximately 1 ton for each 500 S/F of living area.  The policyholder may also be helpful.

Package Units: We have seen more package units over the last several years.  These units have both the air handler and condenser in one unit outside.  You can usually identify these units as they also have duct work connected.

Please remember, we only owe for direct physical loss by or from flood. We do not owe for an inside unit that was not damaged by flood even if the new outside unit will be incompatible with the old inside unit.  (Also, there is no code upgrade coverage in the program)

Split System Units: Same rules apply, we only owe for direct physical loss by or from flood.

Moisture, Mildew and Mold

Our clients do not want to see photos (adjuster photos) with moisture meters.  (mitigation company photos are ok)

If the policyholder risk sits in water for several days and it takes the policyholder several days to gain access and start mitigation, then consideration can be made if mold and mildew rises above the water line.  Good photographs and a detailed narrative report will need to be included to support recommendations above standard operating practices.  The flood program does not pay for Mold Remediation, it pays for water mitigation.  So, if we are making allowances for mold above the water line, we should treat it as though it were wet.  And pay to remove and replace the item.  IE: drywall and wall insulation.

Dry-out & Water Mitigation

Please make sure you are following the FEMA guidelines (W-13025a).  Please see Dry-Out Guidelines in Venue instructions. This information is also in the current claim’s manual (2025).

Drywall

We will continue to remove and replace drywall at 4’ or 8’ depending on the water depth.  Please make sure you are properly addressing the replacement of texture as well.  We recommend texture 1’ above the drywall cut line to blend the new texture with the old.

If the policyholder has only removed 2’ of drywall and plans to replace only the 2’, we should only be replacing the 2’ that has been removed.  You can generally tell by the way the drywall was removed.  A straight cut or the drywall torn out and the edges are ragged.

If we are replacing 8’ of drywall, we need to address the ceiling paint and possibly the ceiling texture.  (due to the tape joint at the meeting of the ceiling and wall)

Wall Texture

We need to document the type of texture on the walls with good photographs.  Our general rule is to replace the texture on only the new drywall and blend 1’ above the new drywall.  There are times that the entire wall may need to be textured due to the type of texture and in those cases, you will need to provide good photographs and a detailed explanation as to why it will be required.

Paint

Our estimates for paint should be two separate line items.  One for the new drywall and one for the existing drywall (when replacing 2’ or 4’ of drywall) For the new drywall, we should Prime/Seal and Paint two coats.  For the existing drywall, we should paint one coat only.

The line item should read as follows:

Seal and Paint walls (two coats) (this is for the new drywall) (this is a total of 3 coats, one seal coat and two paint coats)

Paint Walls (one coat) (this would be the existing drywall)

Missing Walls, cabinets, tubs, etc.

*** Cabinets, tubs, etc. (Material/Paint Reduction)

When calculating the replacement of base/shoe trim, please make sure that you are taking into consideration cabinets, tubs, fireplaces, missing walls, etc.  Same would apply for painting.  Our estimate should reflect this reduction.

Painting Note (Deduction for openings)

We should be deducting all openings in all rooms.  This should show on our estimate and our diagrams.

Painting Cabinets and Paneling)

Most cabinets come prefinished (see Xactimate specs).  We do not need to paint/finish pre-finished cabinets.  If you are estimating a site built or custom cabinet that does need to be finished, then you need to provide good photographs to document what was there.  Also, add a drop down note under the replacement line items to explain the finish.  “unfinished”

This would apply to paneling as well.  Most paneling comes pre-finished (check Xactimate specs).  We do not need to paint/finish pre-finished paneling.  If you are dealing with painted paneling, you need to add a drop down note to the paneling replacement line item “Unfinished” and use the appropriate price.  If prefinished paneling is painted, we do not owe for two finishes, only one.

Doors and door casing

Please make sure you are checking the estimating program specs when writing your estimates. In the last several years, some of the software companies have made some changes and you need to make sure you are not leaving any items off and of course, not duplicating items already included.   Also, just because you buy a pre-hung door at Home Depot and it includes one side of trim, does not mean the software does the same thing.  Please check your specs.

Tile Floors on slab

Tile floors are covered for direct physical loss by or from flood.

Generally speaking, flood waters do not damage tile floors.  The mastic used to install the floors is not water soluble.  Therefore, hollow areas under the tiles were likely present before the flood.  Cracks in the floor are generally caused by settlement, not flood waters.  We should handle each claim on its own merit.

Our clients as well as FEMA/NFIP only allow us to pay for direct physical loss by or from flood.  “There must be evidence of physical changes to the property”.  So, if you cannot see any damage to the tile floor, you cannot include the replacement of the floor in your estimate.  Good photos documenting the damage will be needed to support the replacement of the flooring.  And good photos showing no damage should be included to support your recommendation of denial of the flooring.

Our clients will allow us to re-grout the tile floors.

If the policyholder /contractor is adamant the tile was damaged by flood, we may need to request an engineer (or Tile specialist).  Progressive is very pro-engineer when it comes to tile floors.  They will hire one every time there is a dispute.

General Information

Driver’s License

Principal Residence

Progressive requires a driver’s license on every claim.  This is either to confirm principal residence and/or that we are dealing with the appropriate person.

All other carriers (Except Wright Flood, see below) do not require a driver’s license unless we need to document the risk as the principal residence.

*** The driver’s license must have been issued before the date of loss (and not expired prior to the date of loss) to be considered as valid.

If the loss notice shows a different property address than mailing address or if the loss notice indicates the risk is a secondary residence, and the policyholder is claiming primary residence, then we need to provide evidence the risk is the policyholder’s principal residence.  This would be a situation where you should get a copy of their driver’s license to support the change.

If the risk is not single family or is not insured to value, then it would not qualify for replacement cost, so do not hold up the claim if you only waiting on a valid driver’s license.  Except for Progressive, as they always want the driver’s license.  Even with Progressive, do not hold up the claim for too long (more than a few days).  You can explain in your report that you requested the driver’s license, and it has not been received.

If the policyholder does not have or cannot provide a copy of a valid driver’s license, then a copy of their voter’s registration card may suffice.  After that, we could try a copy of their Homeowner’s policy.  As most companies will not write a Homeowners policy on a non-primary residence.

Please make sure the driver’s license or voter registration card show the property address the policyholder is claiming to be their primary/principal residence.

Please make sure your narrative report discusses whether the risk is the Principal Residence or not.  Also, make sure you understand the difference between Principal and Primary.

Primary is an underwriting term and means the policyholder lives there more than 50% of the time.

Principal is a claims term and is a requirement for replacement cost coverage.  It is defined as the policyholder lives there 80% or more of the time.

Wright Flood requires that we use the “Verification of Primary Residence” form.  Please make sure they the policyholder chooses “80% or more” for their residence to qualify for replacement cost.

APDA

We need to make sure we include an APDA anytime our estimate exceeds 50% of our estimated replacement cost.  If the building is completely destroyed (or washed away) we should complete an APDA immediately.  The APDA needs to be emailed direct to FEMA and a copy should be included with either our preliminary report, a status report or the final report depending on the claim.  Your activity log should document when you emailed the report.  Please only include one policyholder with each APDA submitted.

If the risk is obviously substantially damaged (IE: washed away), our clients are now requesting that we submit a copy of the APDA with our preliminary report.  Since we are now submitting our Prelims through the App, you should submit the APDA same day with a short status report.

The APDA is addressing the full amount of damage, not just the flood damage.  So, if the risk is substantially damaged by wind but has little flood damage, an APDA should still be submitted.

Contents

Please work the contents portion of the claim.  Copying the policyholder’s list into your estimating software is not working it.  You must verify the item was in the risk, verify the item was damaged by flood, and on unusual or big-ticket items, you need to verify the price.

We are seeing claims with 3” of water where the policyholder is claiming the microwave sitting on the counter is damaged, or maybe just the plug of the TV got wet. We must properly address these type items.

On every item, you should consider if it can be cleaned, refinished or repaired before you include it for replacement.

You should include at least a few price verifications on every claim.  This helps show you worked the claim, not just listed items.  Also, any item with a replacement cost more than $2,500.00 should/must include a price verification.  You can ask the policyholder to provide this information with their list.

Just because the policyholder lists an item at a certain price does not mean we should use it.  If we can verify like kind and quality, we should adjust the price and include our supporting documentation.

We need good photos.  We see way too many reports with $50,000.00 plus contents estimates with only a hand full of photos showing damaged contents.  If you pay for 200 pairs of shoes, we need to have photos showing 200 pairs of shoes.  If you pay for a $10,000 piano, we need to have a photo of a $10,000 piano…etc.

We need to break out clothes (and other bulk items), our clients will not accept a lump sum.  For any amount, we need items broken out.

For example, we cannot allow: Men’s Clothes 1 @ $5,500.00

An acceptable breakdown would read:

Men’s Dress Shirts   25 @ 49.50

Men’s Polo Shirts 20 @ 39.95

Men’s Jeans 10 @ 29.99

Men’s Dress Pants 10 @ 85.95

Men’s Suits 5 @ 549.99

Please make sure the contents being claimed match the policyholder’s lifestyle.

Please pay close attention to the age of the items in general.  We have seen claims with $75,000.00 worth of contents replaced in the past 24 months.  If this did happen, we would need to explain how the policyholder was able to pay for this amount in such a short period of time.  An example of this could be a prior flood claim or some other type of loss which resulted in most if not all the policyholder’s contents being destroyed.

Also, as mentioned above, we need photographs to document the loss.  Contents claims are just as important as the building claim and our fees are also based on the total amount of the claim including the contents loss.  Please “WORK” these claims accordingly…  Also, take control of the contents claims earlier on by discussing what is damaged and what is not damaged by flood.  The earlier you have this discussion the better.

Depreciation

Please take proper depreciation on every item.   Everything wears out.  Take the useful life of the item, determine its current age, think about how it was used, what kind of condition was the item in prior to the flood and then take appropriate depreciation.   Please do not take the same percentage of depreciation on every item.   Also, do not use 10% or 20% or 50% as it appears you are using an arbitrary number.

FEMA pays particular attention to contents claims and contents depreciation, please make sure you are paying the appropriate amount and taking appropriate depreciation.

Claims are being kicked back by our clients when they do not feel appropriate depreciation has been taken.  You must explain why you applied the depreciation that you used.  Especially lower than usual depreciation.  You must explain why.

Building Valuation

We need to pay attention to our building valuation and the depreciation we are applying.   We also need to pay attention to the depreciation we are applying on our estimate as it compares to our building valuation.  They should be comparable.  The valuation depreciation should not be 40% and the estimate depreciation average 10%.  Of vice versa.

Make sure to consider the economic age of the property, not just the property’s actual age.   Again, depreciation is based on the age and condition of the property.

RAP Process (NFIP Guidelines)

Depending on the stage of repairs, whether completed or not completed, will determine how you handle the RAP.  Your narrative should address the current status of the repairs.  If the policyholder has not completed repairs, they will need to provide you with documentation to support their request for additional monies.  Such as contractor’s estimates (Contract), etc.  We still need to use judgement as we only owe for reasonable amounts.  Also, we cannot consider any upgrades (code or otherwise) or items that were not damaged by flood.  We only owe for like kind and quality of what the policyholder had at the time of the loss.

A contractor’s estimate is just that, an estimate, and does not mean the policyholder is going to be obligated to pay or even hire the contractor.  We should review the estimate for any omitted items, but we would not change our prices based on an estimate only.  We would need an actual signed contract to consider unit cost increase.

If the policyholder provides an estimate from a PA or Attorney, it does not hold the same weight as an estimate from the policyholder’s contractor.  The PA/Attorney estimate should be reviewed; however, you would use the same standards for consideration as you would have on the original claim.  Look for missed or omitted items or possible s/f discrepancies, but most likely you would not consider any price changes as it is an estimate from a PA, not a contractor.

If the policyholder has completed repairs, we will need a copy of his contractor’s estimate and invoice to determine what if anything additional is owed.  Again, same rules apply, we do not owe for upgrades (code or otherwise), or items that were either not there prior to the flood or were not damaged by the flood.  Make sure the contractor’s estimate is detailed enough to identify what repairs were actually done.  We may also need to request “proof of payment” as well as other documentation discussed in the current claim’s manual.  We are going to need photos of the repairs to confirm it was LKQ, not upgraded.  Progressive

We can always address any items that we omitted on the original estimate.
